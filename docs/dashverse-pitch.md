# Dashverse Pitch  

**Dashverse — Intro Call Playbook**

*Sensing & positioning meeting. Pull, not push. Talk like a person, not a deck.*

*Starting lines, not a script — react to what they say.*

**Review copy for Satyam**

**Executive Summary**

**Purpose of the call.** This is a first introduction and mutual sensing conversation with Dashverse (DashReels microdrama), not a pitch and not a negotiation. The goal is to establish Wiom from a position of strength, let Dashverse lean in on their own, and walk away with a soft "yes, let's test a few titles" — a small, reversible content pilot. We deliberately avoid looking like we're asking for content; we position as the distribution they don't yet have.

**The strengths we showcase.** We do not lead with need — we lead with reach and a paying relationship into an audience Dashverse cannot access today:

  - **Distribution into India-2.** \~1M users a month now, building for the next 500M Indians still without unlimited data — Satyam's vision that access to information is what lets people chase their dreams.
  - **A proven low-price, high-flexibility model.** Wiom Net (₹10 unlimited for anyone) and RWGKN (₹20/day flexible home internet, unlimited, any number of devices) — we already monetize a sachet-spending audience that a ₹499/month model structurally can't.
  - **A new entertainment surface.** Our entertainment platform (for now called Wiom TV) runs across surfaces — mobile, TV, others — with TV as the default UX. A fresh, growing distribution rail, not a crowded one.

**What we bring to the table for Dashverse.** The conversation is framed entirely around value *to them*:

  - **Net-new audience** — viewers who will never pay their ₹499 but will happily watch a few rupees' worth a day. No cannibalization of their premium funnel.
  - **Second life for content** — monetization of titles that have already paid for themselves and are now sitting dark (windowing).
  - **Real viewership signal** — performance data from a price-sensitive segment they've never had eyes on.
  - **Zero-risk entry** — a few titles, one pilot audience, a few weeks. They keep everything, no exclusivity, on our cost.
  - **Top-of-funnel halo** — broad India-2 reach becomes awareness that can feed their premium product later.

**What we want out of it.** Confirm their content portfolio and what's redistributable; agree to a small pilot of a few titles. Commercials, splits, and exclusivity are explicitly deferred to "after the test."

**Posture to hold throughout.** We're qualifying the fit, not seeking approval — we have other content partners already in pilots. We ask more than we pitch, underclaim the product, keep the intro light, and are genuinely fine with a "no."

**1. THE FRAME (hold it quietly the whole call)**

You're not asking Dashverse for content. You're the distribution into an audience they can't easily reach — people who'll spend a few rupees a day but never commit to a ₹499 monthly. You're sitting with them to figure out fit, and you've got other partners already in pilots, so you're relaxed either way. You don't need to *say* any of this. You just hold it. It shows up as: you ask more than you pitch, you don't oversell, and you're fine with a "no."

**One-line internal reminder:** *They're qualifying to get on the rail. Not the other way around.*

**2. OPENING — introduce Wiom, lightly**

Start with us. Keep it short and warm — who we are, what we do, who we do it for, and the why. Don't make it a vision speech; make it a quick, plain intro.

  - **What we are:** *"So, quick intro to us — Wiom's basically making the internet affordable for the part of India that mostly can't afford it today."*
  - **The offering (the two things):** *"We've got two things really. Wiom Net — unlimited internet for anyone for just ten rupees. And 'recharge wala ghar ka net' — flexible recharges on home internet, starts at twenty rupees for a day, unlimited usage, as many devices as you want."*
  - **Who it's for (TG):** *"Our users are the next set of Indians coming online — they don't think in monthly plans, they pay small and flexible, when they feel like it."*
  - **Where we are:** *"We're at about a million users a month right now. Plan's a lot bigger, but that's where we are today."*
  - **Origin / vision (keep it light):** *"The whole thing comes from a belief our founder Satyam has — access to information is what lets people chase their dreams. Right now there are millions of Indians stuck on limited mobile data, so we're building for the next 500 million who still don't have unlimited internet. That's the itch we've been scratching."*

Then pause — let them react or ask. Don't roll straight into the entertainment platform unless they pull you there.

**3. BRIDGE TO THEM — the researched question, then the sachet insight**

Now turn to Dashverse. Don't open with their funnel drop-off (too pointed). Open with what you researched, as a respectful check:

*"Before this, I spent a bit of time on DashReels. From what I could tell, your paid plans start around ₹499 a month, and a bit higher on iOS. Am I right? Is that the only paid content offering you've got, or is there more to it?"*

→ Let them confirm/correct. This is respectful homework, not a trap.

Then the sachet bridge — make it about *your audience's behaviour*, never their funnel failing:

*"Got it. So here's why this is interesting for us. The audience we serve lives life sachet-style — whether it's shampoo, detergent, or their internet recharge, they buy in tiny amounts. A ₹499 monthly commitment just isn't how they spend. But the same person will happily pay a few rupees a day for something they actually like."*

That sets up the fit cleanly: not "your model fails," but "there's a whole audience priced differently than yours." Then let it breathe before going further.

Few other pointers -

1.  90% Indians do not have home internet and hence run out of data. This becomes a bottleneck in expansion even for companies like Meesho beyond a point. No wonder Meesho is an investor.
2.  **\*\*They should understand that our TG is 100% additive to them and bringing them on to platform plus more important sustaining them is where wiom fits in. Low cost\*\*** 

**4. WIOM ENTERTAINMENT PLATFORM — only when pulled, one beat at a time**

Don't deliver these as a block. Drop one when the conversation asks for it.

  - **What it is:** *"On top of all that, we've started building an entertainment platform for these users. We're calling it Wiom TV for now — though it's not really just a TV thing. It runs across surfaces — mobile, TV, others. We say 'TV' because that's the default experience: free live TV that replaces their cable, with short-form stuff on top."*
  - **Status (underclaim it):** *"It's early — genuinely still in pilots. I'd rather not oversell something half-built."*
  - **Why now (practical, not poetic):** *"We're testing content over the next month or so, which is honestly why I wanted to talk now — rather do it while we're actually trying things than in theory later."*
  - **The honest interest:** *"My real question is just — could some of your content work for these users. That's the thing I want to figure out."*

**5. DISCOVERY QUESTIONS — let them reveal, make it feel like their idea**

Sequence matters. Ask, then go quiet. Use "it sounds like…" to reflect back what you hear.

**Open them up:**

  - *"Walk me through how you think about your content — DashReels, the toons, the creation stuff. Where's the energy going right now?"*
  - *"What's actually working best with your audience — the Indian dramas, the AI ones, the C and K dramas? Anything surprise you?"*

**Sourcing (frame it as your homework, not prying):**

  - *"How much of this is yours, made in-house, versus stuff you source or plug in from others? Just trying to understand what's even yours to move around."*
  - *"Sounds like you've actually built a content engine, not just a library — is that right?"*

**The redistribution reveal — make them propose it:**

  - *"If you wanted to reach people who'll never pay your ₹499 but would happily watch every day — what part of your catalogue would you even want out there?"*
  - *"What would have to be true for you to be comfortable with some of your content living on a screen you don't own?"*
  - *"How do you think about stuff that's already made its money in your funnel and is just sitting there now?"*

**Reflect it back so the pilot becomes their idea:**

  - *"So it sounds like the C-dramas and maybe a bit of the AI stuff are what you'd be most okay testing first — that right?"*

**6. THE ASK — a small test, said casually**

*"Honestly, the easiest thing — pick a few titles you're okay with, let us put them in front of one pilot group for a few weeks. You keep everything, nothing exclusive, and you get to see how a totally different audience responds. If nothing happens, no harm. If it works, we figure out the rest. Worth a try?"*

If true, the timing nudge — offhand, not as a line:

*"We're sorting out launch partners over the next few weeks anyway, so the timing kind of works."*

If they hesitate:

*"Is now a bad time to run something small like this?" (Easy for them to say "no, now's fine.")*

**7. REACTIVE FLOW — if they say X, you say Y**

This is how the call will actually go. Don't recite — pick the branch that fits.

**If they ask "So what exactly is Wiom TV / show me the product":**

*"It's early — honestly still in pilots. It's an entertainment platform across surfaces — mobile, TV, others — we just call it 'TV' because that's the default experience. I'd rather not oversell something half-built. What's real today is the audience and the reach. The content layer is what I'm figuring out, which is why I'm here."*

*(Don't over-explain the tech. Underclaiming the product protects the frame.)*

**If they go quiet / "interesting, tell me more":**

*Don't fill it with a pitch. Turn it back: "What's your read — does an audience like that even sound useful to you, or is your whole game the premium funnel?"*

**If they get enthusiastic fast ("yeah this sounds great, let's do it"):**

*Don't bank it — it's a reflexive yes. Pin it down gently: "Love that. What would actually be the first thing — which titles, and who on your side would I work with to get them?" (A real yes has a next action attached.)*

**If they push on money / "what's the revenue split":**

*"Happy to get there — but honestly it's premature. Let's see if the audience even responds first. If it works, the economics are an easy conversation. If it doesn't, we both saved ourselves a negotiation."*

**If they're skeptical / guarded:**

*Don't argue. Label it: "Sounds like you've been burned by distribution deals before, or it's just not a priority right now — either's fine. What's the hesitation?" Then listen.*

**If they say "let me think about it":**

*"Totally. Can I ask what you'd want to see to feel good about even a small test?" (Surfaces the real objection instead of letting it die in a "maybe.")*

**8. OBJECTIONS — the things they won't say out loud**

Keep these plain. Drop the technique-y phrasing.

**"You're tiny and early — is this a waste of our time?"**

*"We are early, fair. That's exactly why it's a small reversible test, not a big bet. We do the work to prove the audience exists, on our cost, you keep every right. Worst case you learn something cheap."*

**"Microdrama is mobile, not TV."**

*"You're right it started on mobile — so mobile's your home with us, the TV thing is just upside. You don't have to buy the TV idea to try the mobile pilot."*

**"Why not just keep it in our own app?"**

*"Because these people aren't in your app and won't be — they're not paying ₹499. So it's not your audience moving, it's a new one you weren't reaching. Your app keeps your payers."*

**"Won't this eat our autopay revenue?"**

*"Shouldn't — different people, different price, different screen. The stuff that's already made its money in your funnel just gets a second life with an audience that was never going to pay you anyway. If anything it's awareness that feeds your premium product later."*

**9. LANDMINES (this specific call)**

  - **Don't over-thank.** Once is enough. Repeated "thanks for your time" reads as needy.
  - **Don't say "we'd love to" / "would you be open to…".** Say *"here's what I'd suggest"* / *"the easiest thing would be…"*
  - **Don't beg for titles.** The content is theirs to offer — pull it with the section-5 questions.
  - **Don't over-explain the tech or the roadmap.** "Early, in pilots" is the whole answer.
  - **Don't feature-dump Wiom.** Intro is light; bring the entertainment platform in only when pulled, one beat at a time.
  - **Don't monologue.** Aim for them talking 60–70% of the time. After \~3 sentences, stop and ask something.
  - **Don't chase a yes.** A quick "sounds great" isn't a deal — the only real yes has a next step attached.
  - **Don't quote terms, splits, or exclusivity.** This is sensing. Push commercials to "after the test."
  - **Say the million-a-month number once.** Repeating it sounds like pleading.
  - **Don't fill silence.** After a question or the ask — wait. The pull lives in the pause.
