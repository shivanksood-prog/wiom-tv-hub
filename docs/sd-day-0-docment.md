# **2. The platform thesis — what you’re actually building**

Wiom’s current customer base is geographically bounded by CSP coverage — home internet only reaches where pipes physically go. The entertainment layer is bounded by phone or TV ownership in Bharat — an order of magnitude larger reach. The wedge captures customer relationships Wiom’s pipes will never serve directly, and those relationships become the substrate for everything else Wiom can offer.

### **The four-layer stack**

|  |  |  |
| :-: | :-: | :-: |
| \*\*Layer\*\* | \*\*What it is\*\* | \*\*Where it lives\*\* |
| \*\*Wiom Internet\*\* | PayG home internet via CSP network | Where pipes are — the existing business |
| \*\*Wiom Entertainment\*\* | The entertainment-layer product line — Wiom TV is the first product; PayG OTT and regional content in time | Mobile, TV, multiple surfaces — reaches anyone with a phone or smart TV |
| \*\*Wiom Distribution\*\* | Distribution layer for OTT partners, regional content, household-fit financial products | On top of the captured relationship |
| \*\*Wiom Platform\*\* | Aggregated Bharat household relationships as a platform substrate | The big bet — what 60 days is laying foundation for |

### **Why the platform is the prize**

  - **Reach expansion:** the entertainment layer reaches Bharat households outside CSP coverage — multiples of Wiom’s current addressable market.
  - **Relationship capture:** a household using Wiom TV daily has a Wiom-shaped relationship even if they’re not on Wiom’s pipes. That relationship is the substrate.
  - **PayG entertainment economics:** OTT’s biggest pain is distribution + billing. Wiom’s “Recharge-wala” positioning solves both at once for partners.
  - **Optionality:** once you hold the relationship, you can offer financial products, regional content subscriptions, anything that fits Bharat household economics.

### **End state vs phase 1 scope**

The full Wiom Entertainment vision covers all three household segments: Wiom-coverage existing customers, Wiom-coverage non-customers (cable households), and outside-coverage households across Bharat. That is the end state.

For your 60-day phase 1, scope narrows deliberately to Wiom-coverage households only. Two reasons:

  - **Removes distribution-channel dependency.** Outside-coverage acquisition requires app-store discovery, social, viral, or paid channels — none of which exist for Wiom TV today. Wiom-coverage households are reachable via CSPs at zero new infrastructure.
  - **Removes ad-spend dependency.** CSPs recruit households in their zones at zero CAC. Outside-coverage at scale requires acquisition spend that isn’t justified before the product is proven.

This is a sequencing choice, not a vision reduction. The platform-relationship-outside-coverage thesis remains the bigger prize; it becomes phase 2 if phase 1 proves the product.

**Critical design principle:** design the product as if outside-coverage households were using it. Don’t take shortcuts that work for Wiom customers but break for everyone else. The product architecture must be vision-shaped; only the recruitment scope is phase-1-shaped.

# **5. Legal classification — lock in Phase 0**

Wiom operates under the PM-WANI framework with VC investors and a CSP partner network. Distributing flagrantly illegal content via Wiom’s brand and platform is existential — it puts regulatory standing, partner relationships, and investor confidence at risk simultaneously. There are operating classifications — pick one and stay within it. Declare in Phase 0; don’t drift. Note: “Tier 3 OTT players” below means small/regional OTT companies (a content partnership path), not the legal Red zone — different concepts.

|  |  |  |  |
| :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| \*\*Classification\*\* | \*\*Content sources\*\* | \*\*Risk profile\*\* | \*\*Acceptable?\*\* |
| \*\*Green — fully licensed\*\* | Doordarshan / Prasar Bharati FTA channels + direct partnerships with smaller channels + AVoD aggregator licenses (MX Player, Watcho) | Zero legal risk; narrower content catalog | \*\*Yes — default\*\* |
| \*\*Amber — grey but defensible\*\* | Public streaming URLs not behind paywalls; embed-permitted streams; regulatory-undefined zones; partnerships with tier-3 OTT players (small/regional OTT companies) | Low–medium risk; broader content catalog | \*\*Yes — with explicit justification per source\*\* |
| \*\*Red — flagrantly illegal\*\* | Scraped premium content, pirated streams, paywall bypass | \*\*Existential risk to Wiom\*\* | \*\*No — off limits\*\* |

### **Tier-3 OTT partnerships — the path worth pursuing**

Small and regional OTT players (“tier-3” in industry parlance — Aha, Sun NXT, Bhakti-focused services, niche regional players) have content but struggle with distribution. Wiom’s pitch to them: we distribute through Wiom TV to first-time internet households across Bharat. The partnership ideal:

  - **No minimum guarantee from Wiom.** No upfront commitment. We don’t pay for inventory we haven’t monetised.
  - **Reverse-revenue-share or freemium models.** Partner content is free for users; Wiom shares with partner when PayG OTT tier kicks in, or partner pays Wiom for the distribution.
  - **Distribution-for-content trade.** Wiom’s reach and brand traded for content depth without licensing fees.

These conversations are yours to lead. Founder-level partnership posture: bring the deal, get approval as needed, don’t wait for a BD team.

# **6. Surface(s) — your call**

Agents let you build all surfaces in parallel — Android phone, iOS, Android TV, Samsung Tizen, LG webOS, Fire TV, web. Choose based on what moves adoption fastest for first-time internet households. Multiple surfaces is allowed; pick the combination you can ship and adopt in 60 days.

### **Candidate surfaces and trade-offs**

|  |  |  |
| :-: | :-: | :-: |
| \*\*Surface\*\* | \*\*Strength\*\* | \*\*Trade-off\*\* |
| \*\*Android phone\*\* | Largest install base in Bharat; Play Store distribution works | Phone is personal, not household-shared; needs cast for TV-on-TV experience |
| \*\*Mobile + cast to TV\*\* | Reaches anyone with a phone; bridges to actual TV via Chromecast / Miracast | Cast setup itself can be friction for first-time-internet users |
| \*\*Android TV / Google TV native\*\* | Native TV-on-TV; no cast needed; remote-control surfacing | Smaller install base in Bharat; app-store approval cycles slower |
| \*\*Samsung Tizen / LG webOS\*\* | Major Bharat smart-TV brands; bundled-in distribution | Closed app stores; certification process is the bottleneck, not build time |
| \*\*Fire TV / Roku stick\*\* | Bypasses TV-brand fragmentation; cheaper than smart TV | Requires hardware purchase by user or Wiom distribution play |
| \*\*iOS\*\* | Premium-tier reach; cleaner casting via AirPlay | Lower share of Bharat first-time-internet households |
| \*\*Web (browser)\*\* | Universal; no install friction | Lower engagement quality vs native; casting more limited |

**How to decide:** test JTBD with target households in Phase 0. Where do they currently watch TV? On what device? What’s the path of least resistance for daily use? Then pick the surface(s) that minimise friction. Agents can build multiple in parallel — don’t artificially constrain yourself to one if covering two is feasible.

# **8. Success criteria — adoption is the progress signal**

**Primary signal — do not let this drift:** real first-time internet households using Wiom TV daily by Day 30 of Phase 2 — across both phase 1 segments. Different intensity bars for each, but daily use is the floor for “this is making progress.”

### **Per-segment bars**

|  |  |  |
| :-: | :-: | :-: |
| \*\*Segment\*\* | \*\*Daily-use bar (Day 30 of Phase 2)\*\* | \*\*Phase-1 success bar\*\* |
| \*\*Wiom-coverage existing customers\*\* | ≥40% daily-active in their household | Pipe data consumption uplift vs baseline |
| \*\*Wiom-coverage non-customers (cable households)\*\* | ≥30% daily-active | \*\*≥15% explicitly inquire about Wiom internet during the test window — the pipe-conversion signal\*\* |

### **Vanity metrics to ignore**

App installs without daily use. Screens built. Channels integrated. Slides produced. Hours of agent code generated. Number of meetings held. These can be high while adoption is zero. Adoption-daily is the only floor that matters.

### **Counter-metric (anti-Goodhart)**

**Household-quoted preference:** by Day 30 of Phase 2, ≥50% of daily-using households say “this is better than what we had before” in a structured interview — not just “I used it.” If usage is high but the household doesn’t feel the product is materially better than their previous TV experience, the adoption is corrupt and the platform thesis is weaker than it looks.
